NEGATIVE OUTCOMES / LIMITATIONS
1. Assumes Quasi-Static Channel Within Each Slot

The entire SINR and coverage analysis assumes the channel g_im stays constant within one time slot (quasi-static assumption)
In reality, for very high UAV speeds, channel can change faster than Δt = 1.34 ms
The paper's constraint Δt ≤ T_c = 1.49 ms is satisfied at tested speeds, but at very high speeds (> 50 m/s) or high carrier frequencies this assumption may break down
Risk: Coverage probability estimates become inaccurate, undermining the entire payoff function π_i


2. NE Uniqueness Not Guaranteed in Sparse Clusters

Theorem 3 (Uniqueness) requires the contraction condition κ < 1
This condition fails for large cluster radii (sparse deployments)
In Figure 9 (fig9_uniqueness), the κ > 1 region shows that for large ρ_k, multiple Nash Equilibria may exist
The paper acknowledges this but does not provide a solution for the non-unique case
Risk: In sparse FANETs, the best-response iteration could oscillate or converge to a suboptimal NE


3. Health Index Weights Are Fixed (AHP-Derived)

The AHP weights (w_E = 0.5, w_T = 0.25, w_L = 0.25) are derived once and fixed
Different mission types may need different weights:

High-speed maneuver missions: link quality (w_L) may matter more
Long-endurance surveillance: energy (w_E) may need even higher weight
High-computation missions: thermal (w_T) may dominate


Risk: Fixed weights may produce suboptimal CH selection in mission types not considered during design


4. DRL Comparison Uses a Simplified Synthetic Model

In Figure 14 (fig14_convergence_drl), the DRL convergence curves are synthetically generated using a logistic function with Gaussian noise rather than running an actual DRL training loop
The SAT values (0.72, 0.61, 0.48 for N = 8, 20, 32) are assumed, not measured from actual DRL training
Risk: This weakens the DRL comparison — a properly tuned DRL implementation might perform better than the paper suggests. Reviewers may challenge this.


5. Reputation Mechanism Relies on Historical Completion Rate Q_j

The incentive-compatibility proof (Theorem 1 of Section VI) requires the discount factor δ to be large enough
In highly dynamic FANETs with short mission lifetimes, the "sufficiently patient" assumption may not hold — clusters may have no long-term stake in reputation
Short missions (single flight, disaster response) mean Q_j history has little weight
Risk: Clusters could misreport their capacity in short missions without facing meaningful reputation penalties


6. No Consideration of UAV Mobility Path Planning

CTRL assumes UAVs hover at a fixed altitude H and positions change due to mobility, but does not optimize UAV trajectories
The cluster centroid q̄_k changes over time but CTRL has no mechanism to reposition UAVs to maintain cluster structure
Risk: Under high mobility (v = 50 m/s), clusters may physically break apart, invalidating the assumed cluster topology and communication range assumptions


7. Synchronization Overhead Not Fully Modeled

The CH election requires all N_k UAVs to broadcast their power p_i^(t) each iteration and receive {p_j^(t)} from all others
At N_k = 10 UAVs and T_conv = 5 iterations, this is 50 beacon exchanges per election
The paper allocates only "2 bytes overhead" per beacon but does not fully account for:

Collision probability when multiple UAVs broadcast simultaneously
Synchronization cost across K = 4 clusters running elections in parallel


Risk: Real-world election overhead may be higher than the theoretical 20 ms estimate


8. Task Model is Simplified (Single Task Type)

The paper models tasks with a single CPU demand τ = 5 × 10^7 cycles
Real FANETs handle heterogeneous tasks: image processing, video streaming, sensor fusion, control commands — each with very different computational profiles, deadlines, and data sizes
Risk: CTRL's utility functions are calibrated for a single task type and may not generalize well to mixed workloads without re-parameterization


9. Limited Real-World Validation

All results are Monte Carlo simulations — no hardware experiments, no real UAV testbed
Key channel parameters (η = 2.2, g_im ~ Exp(1)) are borrowed from existing literature
Real UAV-to-UAV channels may exhibit:

Rician fading (K-factor > 0) instead of pure Rayleigh
Obstructed LoS due to terrain or buildings
Non-stationary interference patterns


Risk: Simulation-to-reality gap may reduce CTRL's performance advantage in deployment


10. Incomplete Sections in the Submitted Paper

Sections B (Research Challenges), C (Contribution) in the Introduction, and Section VII (Solution Approach) appear to be placeholder headings with no content
This suggests the paper is still a work in progress and not yet in final publishable form
Risk: Missing sections weaken the paper's overall argument and will likely require major revision before acceptance


11. Scalability of Offloading Algorithm

The cooperative offloading (Algorithm 2) performs up to 5 redistribution passes per slot
For very large FANETs (many clusters K), the number of feasible neighbor checks grows
The paper only tests up to K = 6 clusters — scalability to K = 10, 20 clusters is unvalidated
Risk: Computational overhead of neighbor evaluation may become significant in large-scale deployments
